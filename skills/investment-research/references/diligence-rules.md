# Diligence Rules

## Universal Questions

- What is the exact asset, company, security, sector, or event under review?
- What decision or research question is the work meant to inform?
- What primary sources are available, and what is missing?
- Which facts are verified, calculated, inferred, or user-provided?
- Which assumptions drive most of the conclusion?
- What evidence would falsify or weaken the thesis?
- What must be monitored after the research is produced?

## Public Company Diligence

- Business: revenue mix, customer concentration, geographic exposure, pricing power, competitive position, and cyclicality.
- Financials: growth, margins, cash conversion, leverage, liquidity, capital allocation, accounting quality, and guidance credibility.
- Valuation: peer set, historical range, scenario assumptions, market-data date, and sensitivity to margins, growth, rates, and multiples.
- Events: earnings, guidance changes, buybacks, dividends, M&A, litigation, regulation, product launches, and management changes.
- Risks: liquidity, crowding, short interest, governance, disclosure quality, and external dependencies.

## Private Investment Diligence

- Market: customer problem, market size, adoption curve, urgency, budget owner, and competitive alternatives.
- Product: differentiation, retention, implementation friction, security, reliability, roadmap, and customer proof.
- Traction: revenue quality, pipeline, churn, cohort behavior, gross margin, sales efficiency, and payback when data exists.
- Team: founder-market fit, hiring gaps, governance, incentives, and execution history.
- Terms: valuation, liquidation preference, dilution, option pool, control rights, information rights, and exit assumptions.

## Credit Diligence

- Cash flow: recurring cash generation, seasonality, working capital, capex, and downside resilience.
- Capital structure: debt layers, maturity schedule, collateral, covenants, guarantees, and ranking.
- Coverage: interest coverage, fixed-charge coverage, liquidity runway, and covenant headroom.
- Recovery: asset value, claims priority, downside scenarios, and refinancing options.

## Sector Or Theme Diligence

- Demand drivers, customer behavior, price sensitivity, regulation, supply constraints, technology shifts, and macro sensitivity.
- Value-chain profit pools and which participants capture or lose economics.
- Structural winners and losers, not only aggregate market growth.
- Evidence that the theme is investable through the target asset, not just broadly attractive.

## Event Diligence

- Distinguish confirmed facts, market expectations, management commentary, and speculation.
- Identify timing, affected financial lines, second-order effects, and what is already reflected in price or consensus when evidence is available.
- Avoid claiming that an event is a catalyst unless timing, mechanism, and uncertainty are clear.
