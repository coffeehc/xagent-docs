# Meeting Brief Rules

## Anti-Meeting Check

Before producing a heavy brief, identify why synchronous discussion is useful. A meeting usually needs at least one:

- tradeoff to resolve
- conflict or disagreement to handle
- co-creation or brainstorming
- relationship-building
- blocker escalation
- decision-maker alignment
- sensitive context that should not be handled only async

If none exists, suggest an async alternative:

- stakeholder update
- written decision brief
- project status note
- comment request
- short approval request

Continue if the user still wants the meeting brief.

## Load-Bearing Inference Gates

Flag low or medium confidence before using it for tactical guidance:

- stakeholder positions
- decision-maker identity
- primary ask
- budget/resource stakes
- relationship tension
- customer/legal/compliance impact
- likely objections

Use labels:

- `[known]`
- `[inferred: high confidence]`
- `[inferred: medium confidence]`
- `[inferred: low confidence, confirm before use]`

## Stakeholder Read

For each important attendee, capture:

- role
- position on topic
- stakes
- likely concerns
- influence
- relationship state
- tactical notes
- confidence

Do not over-psychologize. Use source-backed behavior, stated goals, role incentives, and prior decisions.

## Desired Outcomes

Rank outcomes:

1. Must achieve: meeting is unsuccessful without this.
2. Should achieve: strong preference but not required.
3. Nice to achieve: useful if the conversation allows.

Flat outcome lists hide tradeoffs and make the meeting harder to steer.

## Key Messages

Write key messages as speakable lines:

- short
- direct
- grounded in facts
- connected to the ask
- audience-aware

Avoid slogans, overconfidence, or legal/compliance commitments unless approved language is provided.

## Anticipated Questions

Prepare questions from:

- stakeholder concerns
- missing evidence
- resource implications
- timeline risk
- customer or business impact
- alternative options

Responses should be honest about uncertainty and state what is needed to close gaps.

## Success Signals

Success signals should be observable:

- named decision made
- owner assigned
- date agreed
- blocker removed
- follow-up channel created
- stakeholder objection resolved
- next review checkpoint scheduled

Avoid vague signals such as `alignment feels good`.
