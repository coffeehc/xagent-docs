# Financial Regulatory Impact Rules

## When To Use

Use this reference when a policy affects financial institutions, listed companies, capital markets, investors, funds, financial products, macro liquidity, interest rates, credit supply, disclosure, market structure, or financial compliance.

## Policy Types

Classify the policy:

- monetary policy;
- fiscal policy with financial-market effects;
- prudential regulation;
- conduct regulation;
- market-structure rule;
- securities issuance, IPO, refinancing, delisting, listing, disclosure, or exchange rule;
- banking, insurance, asset-management, fund, broker, payment, fintech, crypto, or digital-asset rule;
- capital requirement, liquidity requirement, leverage rule, reserve requirement, or risk-weight rule;
- investor protection rule;
- product restriction;
- AML, KYC, sanctions, cross-border capital, or data rule;
- enforcement action.

## Affected Financial Actors

Identify who is affected:

- central bank, treasury, regulator, exchange, clearing house, or self-regulatory body;
- banks, brokers, insurers, asset managers, funds, payment firms, fintech firms, exchanges, listed companies, private issuers, rating agencies, auditors, custodians, distributors, retail investors, institutional investors, or foreign investors.

## Transmission Channels

Map financial impact through channels such as:

- funding cost;
- credit supply;
- liquidity;
- reserve requirement;
- capital requirement;
- risk appetite;
- leverage;
- product demand;
- fee income;
- trading volume;
- issuance volume;
- disclosure burden;
- compliance cost;
- provisioning, impairment, or capital consumption;
- valuation multiple;
- earnings sensitivity;
- market access;
- investor eligibility;
- cross-border flow.

## Impact Levels

Separate:

- direct rule change: what immediately becomes required, prohibited, permitted, incentivized, or penalized;
- participant response: how banks, issuers, funds, investors, brokers, or companies may change behavior;
- market reaction: short-term price, volume, liquidity, or sentiment response when evidence exists;
- fundamental impact: revenue, cost, margin, capital, liquidity, risk, business model, or valuation effect over time;
- second-order effect: consolidation, substitution, regulatory arbitrage, product redesign, or competitive redistribution.

Do not conflate short-term market reaction with long-term fundamental effect.

## Financial Impact View

When evidence supports it, identify:

- likely beneficiaries and pressured actors;
- affected sectors and business models;
- revenue, cost, capital, liquidity, margin, or valuation pathways;
- compliance burden and implementation timeline;
- risk level and uncertainty;
- monitoring indicators such as official guidance, implementation rules, regulator speeches, reported volumes, funding rates, credit data, filings, enforcement actions, or company disclosures.

## Checks

- Regulator, jurisdiction, policy type, publication date, effective date, and status are explicit.
- Policy text, market interpretation, and analyst inference are separated.
- Earnings, valuation, liquidity, capital, or risk impact is supported by a visible transmission channel.
- Winners and losers are not asserted without explaining why.
- Market reaction and fundamental impact are separated.
- No trading instruction, personalized investment advice, or guaranteed outcome is presented.
- Compliance-sensitive claims are caveated and routed to qualified human review when needed.
