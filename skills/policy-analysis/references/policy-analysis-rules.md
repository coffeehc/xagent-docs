# Policy Analysis Rules

## Policy Object Classification

Classify the source before analysis:

- official law, regulation, rule, notice, guidance, implementation measure, regulator interpretation, enforcement action, court decision, consultation draft, policy speech, government plan, industry self-regulatory rule, media report, analyst comment, or market rumor;
- jurisdiction and issuing authority;
- publication date, effective date, transition period, expiry date, and current status;
- official source, secondary source, translation, excerpt, or user-provided summary.

Authority level matters. Do not treat drafts, consultations, news reports, or commentary as binding rules unless the source supports that status.

## Extraction Checklist

Extract only what the policy or source supports:

- issuing authority;
- target entities and excluded entities;
- affected industry, geography, product, customer, activity, or transaction type;
- obligations and required actions;
- prohibitions and restrictions;
- incentives, subsidies, approvals, exemptions, or safe harbors;
- penalties, enforcement tools, inspections, disclosure duties, and reporting duties;
- deadlines, transition periods, implementation bodies, and local discretion;
- thresholds, definitions, ratios, eligibility criteria, and calculation bases;
- interaction with existing rules when stated.

## Text Versus Inference

Separate:

- explicit policy text;
- likely implication from the policy mechanism;
- user-provided assumption;
- market or analyst interpretation;
- missing fact;
- point requiring official clarification or legal review.

Use cautious language when the source does not directly answer the question.

## Change Comparison

When comparing against prior state, identify:

- new obligations;
- removed obligations;
- stricter requirements;
- relaxed requirements;
- changed definitions or thresholds;
- changed approval, reporting, disclosure, or enforcement processes;
- changed incentives, subsidies, quotas, or eligibility;
- timeline or transition changes;
- areas where implementation remains unclear.

Avoid saying "major change" unless the affected scope, cost, timing, or enforcement driver is visible.

## Impact Chain

Map impact from rule to consequence:

1. Policy measure.
2. Affected actors.
3. Direct operational impact.
4. Compliance impact.
5. Financial impact when evidence supports it.
6. Competitive or market structure impact.
7. Customer, supplier, product, or channel impact.
8. Second-order effects.
9. Monitoring indicators.

Keep causality explicit. If the chain requires an assumption, state it.

## Materiality Factors

Classify impact as high, medium, low, or uncertain only when evidence supports it. Consider:

- size of affected market or population;
- number and importance of affected entities;
- compliance cost and implementation burden;
- revenue, cost, margin, liquidity, funding, or valuation sensitivity;
- enforcement likelihood and penalty severity;
- timeline and transition period;
- availability of substitutes or workarounds;
- ability to pass through cost;
- policy reversibility;
- conflict with existing business practice;
- uncertainty in implementation guidance.

## Risk And Opportunity Categories

Risks:

- compliance cost;
- business restriction;
- license, approval, or qualification risk;
- margin pressure;
- disclosure or reporting risk;
- enforcement and penalty risk;
- transition or implementation risk;
- uncertainty and interpretation risk;
- market access, supply-chain, customer, or financing risk.

Opportunities:

- subsidies, tax incentives, procurement support, or policy-backed financing;
- demand creation;
- market consolidation;
- higher entry barriers;
- competitive advantage for compliant or better-capitalized actors;
- product, channel, or strategic repositioning;
- clearer rules that reduce uncertainty.
